💧 Ocean Salinity Experiment

Salinity is the measure of dissolved salts in water. Ocean water has an average salinity of 35 parts per thousand (ppt), meaning 35 grams of salt per kilogram of water. This experiment demonstrates how salt affects water density and buoyancy!

Interactive Salinity Meter

Adjust the salinity level: 35 ppt

Materials Needed:

  • Two clear glasses or containers
  • Water (room temperature)
  • Table salt (6-8 tablespoons)
  • Two raw eggs
  • Spoon for stirring
  • Measuring spoons (optional)

Procedure:

  1. Fill both glasses with equal amounts of water (about 3/4 full)
  2. Add 6-8 tablespoons of salt to one glass and stir until dissolved
  3. Leave the other glass with fresh water only
  4. Carefully place one egg in the fresh water glass
  5. Observe that the egg sinks to the bottom
  6. Place the second egg in the salt water glass
  7. Observe that the egg floats in the salt water!
  8. Try adding more salt to make the egg float higher
Why does this happen? Salt water is denser than fresh water. The dissolved salt increases the water's density, making it capable of supporting objects that would normally sink in fresh water. This is why it's easier to float in the ocean than in a swimming pool!

🌙 Understanding Tides

Tides are the rise and fall of sea levels caused by the gravitational forces exerted by the Moon and Sun, and the rotation of Earth. The Moon's gravity pulls on Earth's oceans, creating two tidal bulges - one facing the Moon and one on the opposite side.

Tidal Facts:
  • Most places experience two high tides and two low tides per day
  • Spring tides (highest) occur during full and new moons
  • Neap tides (lowest) occur during quarter moons
  • Tidal range can vary from inches to over 50 feet!

Materials Needed:

  • Large shallow container or tray
  • Water
  • Small ball (to represent Moon)
  • Toy boat or floating object
  • Ruler or measuring stick
  • Notebook for observations

Procedure:

  1. Fill the container halfway with water
  2. Mark the current water level on the side
  3. Place the toy boat in the center
  4. Hold the ball (Moon) close to one side of the container
  5. Observe how the water slightly shifts toward the ball
  6. Move the ball around the container slowly
  7. Watch how the water level changes as the "Moon" moves
  8. Note the boat's position at different "tide" levels
  9. Record your observations about how the Moon's position affects tides

🌊 Wave Formation and Motion

Waves are created primarily by wind blowing across the ocean's surface. The energy from wind transfers to the water, creating ripples that grow into waves. Wave size depends on wind speed, duration, and fetch (distance over which wind blows).

Materials Needed:

  • Large rectangular container or aquarium
  • Water
  • Wooden ruler or stiff cardboard
  • Small cork or floating object
  • Food coloring (optional)
  • Fan or handheld blower (optional)

Procedure:

  1. Fill the container about 3/4 full with water
  2. Let the water settle until completely still
  3. Place the cork on the water surface
  4. Use the ruler to create waves by moving it back and forth at one end
  5. Observe how the cork moves in a circular motion, not forward
  6. Try different speeds and strengths to create different wave sizes
  7. Use a fan to create wind-driven waves across the surface
  8. Notice how faster movement creates bigger waves
  9. Observe wave reflection when they hit the container walls
Wave Energy: While waves move across the ocean, the water itself moves in circular motions. This is why the cork bobs up and down in circles rather than moving forward with the wave. Energy travels through the water, but the water stays mostly in place!

🔄 Ocean Currents

Ocean currents are continuous movements of ocean water driven by wind, temperature differences, salinity, and Earth's rotation. They act like a global conveyor belt, distributing heat around the planet and affecting climate worldwide.

Materials Needed:

  • Clear rectangular container
  • Cold water with ice cubes
  • Hot water
  • Blue and red food coloring
  • Two small cups
  • Dropper or small spoon

Procedure:

  1. Fill the container halfway with room temperature water
  2. Add blue food coloring to ice water in one cup (cold current)
  3. Add red food coloring to hot water in another cup (warm current)
  4. Gently pour some blue ice water at one end of the container
  5. Observe how cold water sinks and moves along the bottom
  6. Carefully add red hot water at the opposite end
  7. Watch how warm water floats on top and spreads
  8. Observe the circulation pattern that forms
  9. This demonstrates thermohaline circulation (density-driven flow)
The Great Ocean Conveyor Belt: This global system of currents takes about 1,000 years to complete one full cycle! It plays a crucial role in regulating Earth's climate by transferring heat from the equator to the poles.

The ocean is divided into five main depth zones, each with unique characteristics. Light penetration, temperature, and pressure change dramatically with depth, creating distinct habitats for marine life.

Amazing Facts:
  • 90% of ocean life lives in the sunlight zone
  • The deepest point is Mariana Trench: 10,994 meters!
  • Pressure at ocean bottom is over 1,000 times surface pressure
  • Some deep-sea creatures create their own light (bioluminescence)
  • We've explored less than 5% of the ocean depths
